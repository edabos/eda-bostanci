a backbreaking job

For a period of over six hours, I had the privilege of accompanying Bahar Yalcin, the manager of the Eiscafé Feldberg, in her daily work. During this time, I was able to engage in in-depth conversations with her and observe her professional activities closely.

This project focuses on information design, a discipline that deals with the creation and visualization of information. It is about transforming complex data and facts into an engaging and understandable form. Through the skillful arrangement of text, graphics, and visual elements, information is clearly structured and easily accessible. The goal of information design is to present complex content in an intuitive and aesthetically pleasing way, and to present it in the form of a brochure.

japanese writing systems

hiragana and katakana

The Japanese writing system, consisting of Kanji, Hiragana, and Katakana, is, in my opinion, a groundbreaking invention with a very fascinating history and an enormous influence on Japanese culture and society.

During the Heian period, a patriarchal society in the 7th century, women were only allowed to learn Manyôgana – an alternative phonetic use of Kanji. Kanji, the Chinese characters, were considered inappropriate because they were too complex for everyday use with a brush. Over time, female scribes simplified the characters and developed Hiragana, which was also known as Onamoji back then. It stands out with its distinctive cursive script and thus became the Japanese script for writing.

Parallel to the development of Hiragana, in the 8th century, the second syllabary of Japan, Katakana, emerged. However, it was originally developed by Buddhist monks as a reading aid for complex texts in Kanji. Katakana is characterized by only 50 characters, which, in comparison to the complex Kanji, almost resembles a form of shorthand. It is therefore much easier to learn and use.
